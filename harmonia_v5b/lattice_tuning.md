Nice question — this is a lovely place where music theory and deep mathematics meet. I’ll explain why the usual Equal Division of the Octave (EDO, e.g. 12-EDO) is *mathematically* unsatisfying when seen through the lenses you asked for — algebraic geometry, field theory, number theory and numerical stability — and then give a concrete, mathematically-grounded alternative that keeps the good musical goals (simplicity, portability) while avoiding the worst consequences of naive equal division.

I'll keep the math precise where it matters and flag when something is a heuristic or an open/technical fact.

# 1) Two ways to think about tuning: multiplicative vs. additive, algebra vs. geometry

Frequencies multiply: to analyze them algebraically we take logarithms (usually base-2) so octave = +1, a ratio r becomes `log2 r` (a real number). So:

* The **multiplicative** group of frequency ratios (positive rationals, or more generally positive real scalars) becomes an **additive** lattice/subspace under logs.
* The space of tunings generated by a finite set of prime factors (2,3,5,7,...) is a **free abelian group** on those primes; after the log map this is a lattice `Z^k` embedded in `R^k` (with coordinates `log2 p` for each prime p).
* From an algebraic-geometry viewpoint the multiplicative world is an algebraic torus (`G_m^k`), and choosing which primes to include is choosing which coordinate directions of that torus we care about.

EDO, however, is different: you pick a single generator `α = 2^(1/n)` and generate the cyclic subgroup `⟨α⟩`. In log space that's a single rank-1 lattice `Z·(1/n)` inside `R`. So EDO collapses the rich multi-prime lattice of just intonation to a one-dimensional arithmetic progression. That collapse is the root of most complaints.

# 2) Field / number-theory view: what you can represent exactly

* **Just intonation** intervals are rational numbers `p/q`. These live in the field `Q` (and their multiplicative closures involve adjoining prime powers, i.e. the multiplicative subgroup of `Q×` generated by the primes you use). The algebraic data needed to represent them exactly are integer exponents of primes.
* **EDO** introduces elements like `2^(1/n)` which are algebraic (they satisfy `x^n − 2 = 0`), so they lie in finite degree algebraic extensions `Q(2^(1/n))` of `Q`. But that field is only of degree `n` and is **generated by one algebraic element**.
* The fundamental mismatch: the multiplicative group generated by primes (3, 5, 7, ...) is **free abelian of rank > 1**. It cannot be embedded isomorphically into a *cyclic* group generated by a single algebraic element. Concretely: there are no integers `a,b` with `2^(a/n) = 3^b` (because `3^b` has 3 in its prime factorization and `2^(a/n)` does not). So EDO can only **approximate** many important ratios, not equal them.

Short: EDO gives you algebraic numbers of a particular one-generator form; just intervals require independent prime exponents — they live in a different algebraic class.

# 3) Transcendence/logarithms: why exact equality is often impossible

Take a musical interval like the perfect fifth `3/2`. In log2 units its size is `log2(3/2) = log2 3 − 1`. Because `3^m ≠ 2^n` for integers `m,n`, `log2(3)` is irrational; hence `log2(3/2)` is irrational. So no finite EDO (which gives only rational multiples of `1/n`) can represent `log2(3/2)` exactly.

A stronger subtlety (brief): classical results (Lindemann–Weierstrass) imply `ln 2`, `ln 3` are transcendental, which shows these logarithms are not simple algebraic rationals. That underlines why relations between prime logarithms are deep number-theoretic objects — you cannot expect one generator like `1/n` to capture them.

# 4) Numerical stability and conditioning: why approximating by EDO can be “unstable”

Think in log space again. You have target vectors `v_p = log2(p)` for primes `p`. You try to represent them by integer multiples of a single step `1/n`. That is a **simultaneous Diophantine approximation** problem. Two issues arise:

*Approximation error is inevitable and non-uniform.* For 12-EDO some intervals are close (fifth ~ 7 steps), some are not (5/4 major third is quite far). These errors are musical: they produce beating, roughness, or altered harmonic relationships.

*Error amplification under operations.* Musical operations (modulation, stacking intervals, inversion) correspond to linear combinations of log vectors. If each atomic interval is approximated with an error `ε_i`, stacking many of them accumulates error; the error can grow linearly with number of operations. In practical terms, when you modulate or stack fifths (Pythagorean chain), the discrepancy (Pythagorean comma) becomes audible or structurally problematic.

*Poor conditioning of single-generator representation.* Representing a `k`-prime lattice with a 1-dimensional approximation is ill-conditioned: small changes in target prime logs map to large changes in required integer multipliers. This is the same idea as an ill-conditioned linear system in numerical linear algebra.

# 5) Concrete numeric examples (12-EDO)

(These are exact computations in cents; 1200 cents = octave.)

* Perfect fifth `3/2`: exact = **701.9550 cents**. 12-EDO fifth = 7×100 = **700.0000** → **error −1.9550 cents** (small, usually acceptable).
* Major third `5/4`: exact = **386.3137 cents**. 12-EDO major third = 4×100 = **400.0000** → **error +13.6863 cents** (clearly audible; explains “EDO third sounds bright / stretched”).
* Minor third `6/5`: exact = **315.6413 cents**. 12-EDO minor third = 3×100 = **300.0000** → **error −15.6413 cents**.
* Septimal 7/4 (a common exotic interval): exact **968.8259 cents**, 12-EDO closest = 10×100 = **1000.0000** → **error +31.1741 cents**.

These figures show the *non-uniformity* of approximation error across harmonics: some low-prime intervals fit well; others are badly distorted.

# 6) Algebraic-geometric picture (short, conceptual)

* Consider the multiplicative torus `G_m^k` corresponding to primes `{2,3,5,...}`. Just intervals are integer points in its character lattice `Z^k`. Equal temperament picks a 1-dimensional subtorus (generated by `2^(1/n)`) and projects the full lattice onto it. Projection is inherently lossy — you collapse orthogonal directions (information about primes other than 2) and create aliasing/approximation errors.

* In geometric terms, EDO is like approximating a `k`-dimensional lattice by a 1-D line and then discretizing that line. The quality of the approximation depends on Diophantine properties (how well the line direction approximates the lattice), hence the number-theoretic depth.

# 7) Practical musical consequences

* Timbre and consonance depend on alignment of partials; EDO misaligns many partials in systematic ways, therefore changing perceived consonance/tone color.
* Modulation: in EDO all keys are nominally “equal,” but because the approximation of intervals is only approximate, the *semantic* identity of chords (how partials align) still changes. Worse, if you try to “fix” intervals in one key to be pure, other keys get worse (the classical temperament trade-off).
* Expressive microtonal relationships (e.g., tunings tuned to 5th and 3rd harmonics) are lost or coarsened.

# 8) Alternative: a mathematically better approach

We want a system that:

1. Represents low-prime (2,3,5, maybe 7) intervals accurately,
2. Is practical (finite set of pitches, portable),
3. Is stable under stacking and modulation in a musically useful set of keys,
4. Has controlled numerical error and good conditioning.

**Proposal: Rank-r lattice temperament (practical “harmonic lattice” method).**

Summary: pick a small set of primes to preserve exactly/near-exactly (typical choice: {2,3,5} or {2,3,5,7}), form their log vectors `v_p = log2 p`. Find a low-dimensional integer basis (rank r, typically r=2) for the lattice that best approximates those vectors under some norm (L∞ or L2). That gives you r generators (e.g., “octave” and a tempered fifth) and a finite keyboard is the projection of the low-index points of the `Z^k` lattice onto your chosen basis.

Concretely:

1. Choose primes P = {2,3,5,7} (the harmonic content you want preserved).
2. Form vectors `v_p = log2 p` in `R^|P|` (these are scalars if you choose base 2; think of them as coordinates in a small dimensional space).
3. Choose desired rank r (usually 2). You want integer matrices `A ∈ Z^{r×|P|}` (the temperament mapping) so that `A · v ≈ w` where `w ∈ R^r` are the real step sizes for your r generators. Equivalently, you solve a simultaneous approximation / lattice reduction problem.
4. Use lattice techniques (LLL or multidimensional continued fractions, or solve a minimax integer optimization) to find `A` that minimizes the worst-case error for the chosen set of prime logs — this gives a temperament with provable small maximum error for chosen primes.
5. The final tuning has r generators; a keyboard is a finite rectangle in `Z^r` (for example, 12×7 grid), each key mapped to a frequency by `f = f0 · 2^{(k·w)}`, where `k∈Z^r`.

**Why this is better**

* You preserve the chosen small primes to very small error, because you solved the approximation problem with them in the objective.
* The representation is **low-dimensional but not 1-D**, so it can represent independent prime directions (e.g., 3 and 5) much better than EDO.
* Numerical conditioning is improved because you solved a simultaneous approximation (small uniform error) rather than forcing everything to be multiples of `1/n`.

**A concrete musical example:**

* `Rank 2 temperament` generated by an octave and a slightly tempered fifth (meantone family) gives excellent 5-limit thirds while keeping fifths acceptable. The 1/4-comma meantone is a classical instantiation of this idea: you temper the fifth slightly so that four tempered fifths exactly equal a pure major third — that is choosing an integer relation to make some small combination exact.

# 9) Algorithm (pseudo-code / recipe you could implement)

```
Input: primes P = [2,3,5,7], rank r=2, keyboard size bounds
Compute v = [log2(p) for p in P]           # target prime log vectors
Find integer matrix A (r × |P|) that minimizes max_{p in P} | A[:,p]·w - v[p] |
  # equivalently, find small integer relations among the v's using lattice reduction:
  - build lattice basis from columns [M * v, I] with large scaling M
  - run LLL to find short relations
  - interpret short vectors as integer linear combinations A
Solve for real generator sizes w (least squares or constrained optimization)
Construct keyboard as finite subset of Z^r and map to frequencies f = f0 * 2^{k·w}
Output: frequencies, generator step sizes, and mapping
```

Comments: pick objective L∞ to control worst audible error (max cents), or L2 to minimize total squared error. Use standard libraries (LLL) to search small integer relations.

# 10) A concrete small system you can try now

* Pick `P={2,3,5}` and `r=2`. Solve for two generators `g1` (octave fixed = 1), `g2` (a tempered fifth as a real number of octaves) that minimize max error for `3/2` and `5/4`. This is exactly the classical 1/4-comma meantone idea: temper the fifth so that 4 fifths ≈ major third. The result gives major third ≈ pure 5/4 within ≈ 0.5–1 cent while fifths are ≈ −5–6 cents from pure — a musically pleasant tradeoff.
* If you want more uniform coverage across more primes (e.g. include 7), increase r to 3 or choose a larger finite keyboard (so more pitches available).

# 11) Takeaway / short moral

* EDO is elegant (uniform, modular, easy to implement) but **mathematically it throws away the multi-prime algebraic structure of harmonic ratios** by collapsing everything onto a single cyclic generator. That causes unavoidable approximation error, poor conditioning for some musical operations, and non-uniform distortion of harmonics.
* A better systematic approach is to treat tuning as a **lattice approximation problem**: select which primes/harmonics matter, choose a small rank (2 or 3) that gives musical simplicity, and compute the best integer relations (via lattice reduction) to get generators that minimize the worst audible error. This produces a practical, portable temperament that is *provably* better at representing the harmonics you care about than a blind EDO.
